Following our earlier discussion about whether Singapore’s Critical Core Skills framework fully captures the skills needed in the Australian context, FSO has undertaken a desktop review of more than 66 workforce and skills documents against the framework.

The review included all JSC workforce plans published on their websites, relevant Jobs and Skills Australia reports, World Economic Forum reports and other cross-industry workforce and skills research. We have summarised the findings below and would value JSA’s views, particularly given the Skills Harvester work and JSA’s broader exploration of these skills and capabilities.

Some terms may map to more than one skill depending on context. For example, critical thinking may sit under Sense Making or Problem Solving, while leadership may sit under Developing People or Influence.

The review also identified broader capabilities that appear across the Australian workforce and draw on several Critical Core Skills, but may not be fully covered by the framework.

Capability	Why it may require further analysis
Leadership and people management	CCS covers developing and influencing people, but not the full responsibility for team direction, delegation, performance and workforce outcomes.
Project management	CCS supports planning, collaboration and problem solving, but does not cover scope, schedule, resources, governance, project risk and benefits.
Business acumen	CCS supports customer focus, sense making and decision making, but does not fully cover value creation, commercial drivers, business models and organisational performance.
Change management	CCS covers adaptability and influence, but not change impacts, readiness, resistance, adoption and implementation.
Strategic thinking	CCS supports sense making and decision making, but does not fully cover setting direction, assessing scenarios, strategic choices and aligning priorities.
Risk management	CCS supports risk-based judgement, but does not cover the full process of identifying, assessing, treating, monitoring and governing risk.
